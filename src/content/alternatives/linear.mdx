Linear redefined what issue tracking could feel like for software teams. Its speed, keyboard-first design, and opinionated workflows made Jira feel like legacy software overnight. But Linear is not the right fit for every team. Whether you need more customization, tighter budget control, deeper integrations with non-engineering workflows, or self-hosting capabilities, several alternatives deserve serious consideration.

## Why look for a Linear alternative?

- **Pricing for larger teams** — Linear's per-seat pricing ($8/user/month) adds up, especially when you want to include non-engineering stakeholders like designers, PMs, and QA
- **Customization limits** — Linear is opinionated by design, which is a strength until you need a workflow it does not support
- **Self-hosting requirements** — Regulated industries or security-conscious organizations may need to run their project management tool on their own infrastructure
- **Cross-functional needs** — Linear is built for engineering teams, but many organizations need a single tool that works for marketing, design, operations, and engineering
- **Integration depth** — While Linear has good integrations, some teams need deeper connections to specific tools in their stack

## Top alternatives

### 1. Jira

**Best for:** Large organizations that need enterprise-grade customization and compliance

Yes, Jira is the tool Linear was built to replace. But dismissing Jira entirely would be a mistake. Atlassian has invested heavily in modernizing the experience, and Jira's latest interface is dramatically better than the version that gave it its reputation. For large organizations with complex workflows, compliance requirements, and cross-team dependencies, Jira still offers capabilities that no competitor can match.

Jira's strength is its depth. Custom fields, workflow automations, advanced roadmaps, cross-project reporting, and a massive marketplace of plugins mean you can model virtually any process. If you are a 500-person engineering organization with regulatory requirements, Jira's audit trails, permission models, and Atlassian Guard integration are hard to replicate elsewhere.

The trade-off is clear: Jira is more complex to set up and maintain. It requires an admin who understands the system, and the default configuration is not great. But a well-configured Jira instance is genuinely productive.

**Pricing:** Free for up to 10 users. Standard at $8.15/user/month. Premium at $16/user/month. Enterprise pricing available.

**Pros:**
- Unmatched customization for workflows, fields, and reporting
- Massive ecosystem of plugins and integrations
- Advanced roadmaps and cross-project dependency tracking
- Enterprise compliance features (SOC 2, FedRAMP, HIPAA eligible)

**Cons:**
- Significantly more complex to configure and maintain than Linear
- Performance can be sluggish, especially with heavily customized instances
- The interface, while improved, still feels heavier than Linear
- Feature bloat — most teams use a fraction of what Jira offers

### 2. Shortcut (formerly Clubhouse)

**Best for:** Mid-sized engineering teams that want Linear's simplicity with more flexibility

Shortcut occupies a sweet spot between Linear's minimalism and Jira's complexity. It offers a clean, fast interface with features like Stories, Epics, Milestones, and Iterations, organized in a way that feels natural for software development. Shortcut is particularly good for teams that have outgrown simpler tools but do not want the overhead of Jira.

One area where Shortcut genuinely excels is its API and integrations. The API is well-documented and covers virtually every feature, making it straightforward to build custom automations and connect Shortcut to your internal tools. The built-in Slack integration is also excellent — you can create stories, update statuses, and get notifications without leaving Slack.

Shortcut also handles the "docs alongside issues" problem well with its Docs feature, letting teams keep specs, RFCs, and notes in the same tool as their issues without needing a separate wiki.

**Pricing:** Free for up to 10 users. Team plan at $8.50/user/month. Business at $12/user/month. Enterprise pricing available.

**Pros:**
- Clean interface that strikes a balance between simplicity and power
- Excellent API and Slack integration
- Built-in Docs feature for specs and notes alongside issues
- Milestones and Iterations for planning without the overhead of Jira

**Cons:**
- Smaller community compared to Jira or Linear
- Mobile app is functional but not as polished as Linear's
- Fewer keyboard shortcuts and speed optimizations than Linear
- Some advanced reporting features are only available on higher tiers

### 3. Plane

**Best for:** Teams that want an open-source, self-hostable Linear alternative

Plane is the most compelling open-source alternative to Linear. It looks and feels remarkably similar — fast, clean, with a modern UI that would not be out of place in a YC startup. But unlike Linear, you can self-host Plane on your own infrastructure, giving you complete control over your data.

For teams in regulated industries, government organizations, or companies with strict data residency requirements, Plane solves a real problem. You get a modern issue tracker without sending your project data to a third-party cloud. The self-hosted version is fully featured and backed by Docker Compose for straightforward deployment.

Plane also offers a cloud-hosted version if you do not want to manage infrastructure. The feature set covers issues, cycles (sprints), modules (epics), views, pages (docs), and analytics — covering the core workflow that most engineering teams need.

**Pricing:** Free and open source for self-hosting. Cloud free tier available. Pro at $7/user/month.

**Pros:**
- Open source with full self-hosting option
- Modern UI that feels as fast as Linear
- Active development with frequent releases
- Lower per-user cost than Linear on the cloud plan

**Cons:**
- Self-hosting requires infrastructure management and maintenance
- Feature set is still catching up to Linear in some areas (e.g., triage, SLA tracking)
- Smaller ecosystem of integrations
- Community is growing but still smaller than established tools

### 4. GitHub Issues + GitHub Projects

**Best for:** Small teams and open-source projects already living in GitHub

GitHub has significantly upgraded its project management capabilities. GitHub Projects provides kanban boards, tables, timelines, and custom fields — all tightly integrated with GitHub Issues and Pull Requests. For teams whose entire workflow already lives in GitHub, using Projects eliminates context switching entirely.

The deep integration is the killer feature. Issues automatically update when PRs are merged, project boards reflect the real state of your code, and everything is linkable through GitHub's unified interface. For open-source projects, this is especially valuable since contributors are already on GitHub.

The downside is that GitHub Projects is still less capable than dedicated tools for roadmap planning, sprint management, and cross-team coordination. It works best for smaller teams or as a lightweight layer on top of your development workflow, not as a full-featured project management suite.

**Pricing:** Free for public repositories. GitHub Team at $4/user/month. Enterprise at $21/user/month.

**Pros:**
- Zero context switching for GitHub-centric teams
- Direct connection between issues, PRs, and project boards
- Included in existing GitHub subscription
- Excellent for open-source project management

**Cons:**
- Limited roadmap and capacity planning features
- No sprint velocity tracking or advanced analytics
- Not suitable for non-engineering stakeholders
- Custom fields and automations are less powerful than dedicated tools

### 5. Notion (as a project tracker)

**Best for:** Cross-functional teams that want one tool for docs, wikis, and project management

This is an unconventional pick, but Notion's database features have matured to the point where many teams successfully use it as their primary project tracker. With views (kanban, table, timeline, calendar), relations between databases, rollups, formulas, and automations, you can build a surprisingly capable issue tracking system inside Notion.

The advantage is consolidation. Your specs, meeting notes, roadmaps, and issues all live in one tool. For cross-functional teams where engineering, design, product, and marketing all need visibility into project status, Notion's flexibility is hard to match. Everyone already knows how to use it, and you avoid the "engineers use Linear, PMs use something else" problem.

The trade-off is performance and structure. Notion databases become slow with thousands of items, and without the guardrails of a purpose-built tool, your project tracking setup can become messy over time. It works best for teams under 50 people who value flexibility over prescribed workflows.

**Pricing:** Free tier available. Plus at $10/user/month. Business at $15/user/month.

**Pros:**
- Combines docs, wikis, and project tracking in one tool
- Accessible to non-technical team members
- Highly flexible with custom views, filters, and relations
- Eliminates the need for separate documentation tools

**Cons:**
- Performance degrades with large databases
- No built-in Git integration or developer-specific features
- Lacks purpose-built features like cycle tracking and triage queues
- Requires discipline to maintain a clean project structure

## Our recommendation

If you love Linear's speed and design philosophy but need self-hosting, **Plane** is the obvious choice — it captures the same spirit while giving you full control over your data. For mid-sized teams that find Linear too rigid and Jira too complex, **Shortcut** hits the sweet spot with a clean interface and more flexibility than Linear offers. If your organization is large enough to need enterprise compliance, advanced reporting, and deep customization, **Jira** remains the most capable tool despite its reputation — just invest in proper configuration. And for small teams that are already all-in on GitHub, do not overlook **GitHub Projects** — it is free, integrated, and increasingly capable for lightweight project tracking.
