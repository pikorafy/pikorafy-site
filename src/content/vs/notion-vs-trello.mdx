Notion and Trello are two of the most popular tools for managing tasks and projects, but they take fundamentally different approaches. Trello is a focused kanban board tool — simple, visual, and immediately intuitive. Notion is an all-in-one workspace that includes kanban boards alongside databases, wikis, docs, and a growing AI layer. Choosing between them depends heavily on how complex your workflows are and what else you need from your productivity stack.

## Overview

**Notion** is a flexible workspace where you can build databases, documents, wikis, and project boards. Everything lives in one place, and you can connect content across different views and databases. It suits teams who want a single tool for knowledge management and project tracking.

**Trello** is a dedicated kanban board application. Cards move through columns, Power-Ups extend functionality, and the interface is intentionally simple. It is excellent for visual thinkers who want to see work status at a glance without configuration overhead.

## Key differences

### Ease of getting started

Trello wins here clearly. Open Trello, create a board, name your columns, start adding cards. You are productive within five minutes. Notion requires more setup — you need to decide how to structure your workspace, create a database, configure views, and understand the relationship between pages and blocks.

For teams that want to be up and running immediately, Trello's simplicity is a genuine advantage.

### Flexibility and depth

Notion is far more flexible. A Notion database can be viewed as a kanban board, but also as a table, calendar, gallery, timeline, or list. You can add custom properties to cards (text, numbers, dates, selects, people, checkboxes, formulas), link databases together, and filter views by any combination of properties.

Trello's cards can have checklists, attachments, due dates, labels, and member assignments — but the data model is simpler. You cannot create calculated fields, link cards across boards natively, or switch between multiple layout views without Power-Ups.

### Task management features

Both tools handle core task tracking, but Notion's database system makes it easier to manage complex workflows with many properties and relationships. Trello's kanban view is more focused and less cluttered by default — better for teams who just want to see what is in progress.

Trello's automation (Butler) handles common repetitive actions well: moving cards, sending notifications, and updating fields when conditions are met. Notion's automation is less mature, though it has improved.

### Knowledge management

Notion is in a different category here. Trello cards are task containers — not great for storing long-form documentation. Notion's pages can be rich documents, meeting notes, wikis, and project briefs, all linked to relevant tasks. If you want your project boards and your team knowledge base to live in one place, Notion is the clear choice.

### AI features

Notion AI has become one of the most capable writing and productivity AI tools built into any workspace. It can summarize pages, draft content, auto-fill database properties, and answer questions about your Notion workspace. Trello does not have comparable AI features.

### Integrations

Both tools integrate with popular services like Slack, Google Drive, and GitHub. Trello's Power-Ups system extends its functionality significantly — there are hundreds of integrations, and the calendar Power-Up, time-tracking integrations, and voting features are genuinely useful. Notion's integrations are available on paid plans and cover most major tools.

## Pricing comparison

| Plan | Notion | Trello |
|------|--------|--------|
| Free | Yes (limited pages/blocks) | Yes (up to 10 boards) |
| Entry paid | $10/member/month (Plus) | $5/user/month (Standard) |
| Business | $15/member/month | $10/user/month (Premium) |
| Enterprise | Custom | Custom |

Both tools have usable free tiers. Trello's free plan is generous for small teams with simple needs — 10 boards covers most solo users and small teams. Notion's free plan has limitations on blocks and history that you hit fairly quickly if using it as a main workspace.

## Pros and cons

### Notion

**Pros:**
- All-in-one workspace (docs, wiki, database, project management)
- Multiple views of the same data (kanban, table, calendar, timeline)
- Notion AI for summarization, drafting, and property filling
- Highly flexible data model with custom properties and formulas
- Strong for knowledge management alongside project tracking

**Cons:**
- Steeper learning curve
- Can become complex and disorganized without intentional structure
- Automation less mature than dedicated project management tools
- Performance can be slow on very large workspaces
- Offline support is limited

### Trello

**Pros:**
- Extremely easy to learn and use
- Clean, visual kanban experience
- Excellent mobile apps
- Butler automation handles common workflows well
- Large ecosystem of Power-Ups
- Better for teams who just want kanban without complexity

**Cons:**
- Limited to kanban view without Power-Ups
- No built-in timeline or calendar without Power-Ups
- Weaker for knowledge management and documentation
- Card data model is simpler and less flexible
- No meaningful AI features

## When to use each

**Choose Notion if:**
- You want one tool for project management and team knowledge
- Your workflows have complex data that benefits from custom properties
- You need multiple views of the same data set
- AI-assisted writing and summarization would be useful to your team
- You are a small team using Notion as your primary workspace already

**Choose Trello if:**
- You want the simplest possible kanban tool
- Your team is less technical and needs immediate usability
- Your workflows are straightforward with clear stages
- Mobile is a primary use case
- You are already in the Atlassian ecosystem (Jira, Confluence)

## The verdict

**Trello** is the better choice if simplicity and visual clarity are your priorities. It is excellent for straightforward project tracking, personal task management, and teams who want a tool that requires minimal setup and maintenance.

**Notion** is the better choice if you want an integrated workspace where project management, documentation, and team knowledge all coexist. The additional complexity pays off for teams who invest in structuring their workspace well.

For many teams, the best answer is actually both: Notion for documentation and knowledge management, and a focused tool like Trello or Linear for task tracking. But if you want to minimize your tool count, Notion's flexibility covers Trello's use cases — while Trello cannot cover Notion's.
